Rose plant

Botanical Information:

  • Scientific Name: Rosa
  • Family: Rosaceae
  • Genus: Rosa
  • Species: There are over 100 species of roses, with thousands of cultivars and hybrids

Material you"ll needs

  • Rootstock rose plant (the base onto which you'll graft)
  • Scion (the cutting you'll graft onto the rootstock)
  • Grafting knife or sharp utility knife
  • Rubber bands or grafting tape
  • Wax or grafting compound (optional)
  • Sterilizing solution (like rubbing alcohol)
  • Clean cloth or tissue
  • Pruning shears

Steps:

1. Choosethe Right Time: Grafting is usually done in early spring when the plants are coming out of dormancy and before they start actively growing.

2. Select Rootstock and Scion: Choose a healthy rootstock rose plant and a scion (cutting) with at least one bud or eye. The scion should be taken from a rose variety you want to propagate.

3. Prepare Tools: Make sure your grafting knife or utility knife is sharp and clean. Sterilize the blade with rubbing alcohol or a similar sterilizing solution to prevent disease transmission.

4.Make the Cuts: On the rootstock, make a slanting cut about 1-2 inches above the soil line. On the scion, make a matching slanting cut with a bud/eye attached. This cut should mirror the cut on the rootstock.

5. Join the Cuts:Place the scion onto the rootstock's cut, aligning the cuts and making sure the cambium layers (the green tissue just below the bark) of both pieces are in contact. This is essential for successful grafting.

6. Secure the Graft:Use rubber bands or grafting tape to hold the scion firmly in place on the rootstock. The idea is to ensure good contact between the two surfaces. Some gardeners also apply a thin layer of wax or grafting compound over the cut area to seal it and prevent drying out.

7. Protect the Graft:If desired, you can place a plastic bag or a clear plastic container over the grafted area to create a mini greenhouse. This helps maintain humidity and promotes healing.

8. Wait and ObservePlace the grafted plant in a sheltered location with indirect sunlight. Over the next few weeks, monitor the graft to see if new growth emerges from the scion. This indicates a successful graft.

9. Remove Bands/Tape: Once you see new growth, carefully remove the rubber bands or grafting tape. Make sure not to damage the new growth.

10. Transplanting:Once the graft has successfully grown and is sturdy, you can transplant it into a larger container or directly into the garden.

About Us

Grafting or graftage[1] is a horticultural technique whereby tissues of plants are joined so as to continue their growth together. The upper part of the combined plant is called the scion (/ˈsaɪən/) while the lower part is called the rootstock. The success of this joining requires that the vascular tissues grow together.
